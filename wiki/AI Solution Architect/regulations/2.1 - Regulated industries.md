# AI in Regulated Industries

*From the perspective of an AI Solution Architect*

Regulated industries (healthcare, finance, pharma, energy, manufacturing with safety standards) impose constraints that cut across all phases of the [[technical-framework]] and all 4 [[Overview]] dimensions. The points below are the ones that most often block or derail projects.

---

## 1. Regulatory Risk Classification

**Problem**: Before scoping anything, the AI system must be classified under applicable regulation. The EU AI Act categorizes systems as unacceptable risk (banned), high-risk (strict requirements), limited risk, or minimal risk. Most use cases in healthcare, finance, and safety-critical manufacturing fall into high-risk — triggering a long list of mandatory requirements that take months and significant budget to satisfy.

**Solution**: Make risk classification the first step of Phase 1 (problem framing). Identify which regulation applies (EU AI Act, FDA 21 CFR Part 11, MDR, SR 11-7 for banking) and which risk tier the use case falls into. If high-risk, flag the additional cost and timeline to the business unit before committing to the project.

---

## 2. Explainability

**Problem**: Regulators and auditors require that decisions can be explained in human-readable terms. Neural networks are inherently opaque — a defect classifier or credit scoring model that cannot explain *why* it flagged something is often not approvable, regardless of its accuracy.

**Link to dimensions**: Directly constrains **model architecture** (dimension 4). Neural networks need additional explanation layers; linear and tree models are inherently more interpretable.

**Solution**: Prefer inherently interpretable architectures (linear models, tree models with SHAP values) where the regulatory bar is high. For neural networks, apply post-hoc explainability methods (SHAP, LIME, Grad-CAM for images). Document model behavior systematically in a model card. Build explainability into the acceptance criteria from day one, not as an afterthought. → [[Explainability]]

---

## 3. Human Oversight

**Problem**: The EU AI Act and most sector regulations prohibit fully automated high-stakes decisions without a human in the loop. A classification model that automatically rejects a loan, blocks a patient from treatment, or stops a production line without human review may not be legally permissible.

**Link to dimensions**: Directly constrains **capability** (dimension 1). Decision making AI and automated classification systems carry the highest oversight requirements. The more autonomous the capability, the higher the regulatory bar.

**Solution**: Design for *decision support* rather than *decision replacement* by default in regulated contexts. The model surfaces a recommendation and confidence score; a qualified human approves or overrides. Build escalation paths and override logging into the system architecture from the start.

---

## 4. Auditability & Traceability

**Problem**: Every AI decision that affects a regulated outcome must be traceable after the fact — which model version made the decision, on which input data, at what time, with what confidence, and who reviewed it. Without this, a post-incident audit or regulatory inspection cannot be satisfied.

**Solution**: Implement full MLOps logging from day one: model registry with version pinning, structured input/output logging for every inference call, data lineage tracking from raw source to training set, and an audit trail for human overrides. This is infrastructure work that must be scoped into the project, not added later.

---

## 5. Data Privacy

**Problem**: Training data in regulated industries often contains personal or sensitive data (patient records, financial transactions, employee data). GDPR and sector-specific regulations restrict how this data can be collected, stored, moved, and used for model training.

**Link to dimensions**: Directly constrains **learning paradigm** (dimension 3). Supervised learning requires labeled data — but that data may be legally restricted or require anonymization that degrades label quality.

**Solution**: Apply data minimization and anonymization before training. Where data cannot leave the source system, use **federated learning** (model trains locally, only gradients are shared). For scarce or restricted data, consider **synthetic data generation** (train a generative model on approved data, generate synthetic samples for the ML model). Document the legal basis for every data source used in training.

---

## 6. Data Quality & Labeling Standards

**Problem**: In regulated industries, training data itself must meet quality standards — it is not enough for the model to be accurate if the labels were produced inconsistently. For example, medical image labels must be produced by qualified clinicians, and inter-rater agreement must be measured and documented.

**Link to dimensions**: Affects **learning paradigm** (dimension 3). Supervised learning is only as good as its labels — in regulated contexts, label quality is a compliance requirement, not just an ML concern.

**Solution**: Define a formal labeling protocol before data collection begins: who is qualified to label, how disagreements are resolved, what agreement metric is required (Cohen's kappa is standard). Implement data quality gates in the ML pipeline that reject training runs below the quality threshold. Keep a versioned, immutable record of the training dataset.

---

## 7. Model Validation & Regulatory Approval

**Problem**: In some industries (pharma, medical devices, aviation), AI models must pass a formal validation process — independent from the development team — before they can be deployed. This is distinct from standard ML evaluation. It is slow, documentation-heavy, and requires the model to be frozen: retraining triggers re-validation.

**Solution**: Plan validation as a separate project phase with its own timeline and budget. Use a validation framework aligned to the applicable standard (e.g., GAMP 5 for pharma software, IEC 62304 for medical device software). Freeze the model version at the start of validation. Run parallel operation (AI alongside the existing process) as evidence before full go-live.

---

## 8. Model Drift & Re-validation After Retraining

**Problem**: Models degrade over time as the real world shifts (new products, process changes, economic shifts). In unregulated contexts you simply retrain. In regulated contexts, retraining may trigger a full or partial re-validation — adding cost and delay every time the model is updated.

**Link to dimensions**: Affects **learning paradigm** (dimension 3). Supervised models trained on historical data are most vulnerable to drift. The more frequently the underlying process changes, the higher the re-validation burden.

**Solution**: Design the monitoring and retraining strategy with the regulatory team before go-live. Agree upfront on what constitutes a significant model change (requires re-validation) vs. a minor update (requires only regression testing). Use drift detection metrics (Population Stability Index, KL divergence on input distributions) to trigger alerts. Consider a modular architecture that isolates the regulated model component so that peripheral changes do not trigger full re-validation.

---

## 9. Bias & Fairness

**Problem**: Models trained on historical data encode historical biases. In regulated industries, biased outputs can constitute discrimination — rejected loan applications, unequal treatment in healthcare triage, biased hiring recommendations. Regulators (especially in finance and HR) are actively auditing for this.

**Link to dimensions**: Most relevant for **Predictive AI → classification** (dimension 1) where the output affects individuals belonging to protected groups (gender, age, ethnicity).

**Solution**: Conduct a bias audit before deployment: measure fairness metrics (demographic parity, equalized odds, calibration) across protected groups. Document findings and mitigations. Re-run the audit after every retraining cycle. Ensure training data is representative of the population the model will be applied to.

---

## 10. Third-Party & Vendor Model Risk

**Problem**: Using foundation models or vendor APIs (e.g., Azure OpenAI, AWS Bedrock) means the model is a black box owned by a third party. The vendor can change or deprecate the model, and you have limited insight into training data or model behavior. Regulators may not accept a system where the SA cannot fully document what the model was trained on.

**Link to dimensions**: Most relevant for **Generative AI / RAG** (dimension 1) using cloud-hosted LLMs, and **Self-supervised** learning paradigm (dimension 3) where the foundation model is externally managed.

**Solution**: Assess vendor risk formally: contractual SLAs for model stability, right to audit, data processing agreements. For the highest-risk use cases, prefer self-hosted open-source models (Llama, Mistral) over vendor APIs — you control the version and the data. Where vendor APIs are used, implement a model version pinning strategy and test every vendor update against your validation suite before rollout.

## Summary: Where Regulated Industry Constraints Hit the Framework

| Framework phase | Key regulatory constraint |
|---|---|
| Phase 1 — Problem framing | Risk classification (EU AI Act tier), human oversight requirements |
| Phase 2 — Data assessment | Data privacy (GDPR), labeling standards, data quality gates |
| Phase 3 — AI dimension mapping | Explainability constrains architecture; data restrictions constrain learning paradigm |
| Phase 4 — Architecture & integration | Audit logging, model registry, input/output traceability |
| Phase 5 — Risk & feasibility | Formal validation process, timeline and budget impact |
| Phase 6 — MLOps & monitoring | Drift detection, re-validation triggers, bias monitoring |
