# EU AI Act

*Relevant points for an AI Solution Architect Consultant*

The EU AI Act (in force August 2024, most provisions apply from August 2026) is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. It applies to any AI system placed on the EU market or affecting EU citizens — regardless of where the provider is based.

---

## Core Concept: Risk-Based Tiering

The Act classifies every AI system into one of four risk tiers. Your first job on any engagement is to determine which tier the use case falls into.

| Tier | Description | Examples | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Unacceptable risk** | Banned outright | Social scoring by governments, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces, subliminal manipulation | Cannot be built or deployed |
| **High risk** | Allowed but heavily regulated | AI in medical devices, credit scoring, recruitment, critical infrastructure, safety components in manufacturing | Full compliance obligations (see below) |
| **Limited risk** | Transparency obligations only | Chatbots (must disclose it's AI), deepfakes | Disclose AI involvement to the user |
| **Minimal risk** | No obligations | Spam filters, AI in video games | Free to deploy |

> **SA rule of thumb**: If the AI output directly affects a person's access to services, safety, or rights — assume high risk until proven otherwise.

---

## High-Risk Obligations — What They Mean in Practice

If a use case is high-risk, these requirements apply before deployment. Each one has direct SA implications:

**1. Risk management system**
A documented process to identify, assess, and mitigate risks throughout the AI system's lifecycle — not a one-time checklist. Must be maintained and updated. *SA impact: scope this as a standing workstream, not a project deliverable.*

**2. Data governance**
Training, validation, and test datasets must be documented, relevant, representative, and free of errors as far as reasonably possible. Bias must be assessed. *SA impact: data lineage and dataset documentation are compliance requirements, not nice-to-haves.*

**3. Technical documentation**
A detailed technical file describing the system — purpose, architecture, training data, performance metrics, known limitations. Must be kept up to date. *SA impact: model cards and system documentation must be produced and versioned alongside the model.*

**4. Transparency & instructions for use**
The system must come with documentation for the deployer (the business using the AI) explaining capabilities, limitations, and human oversight requirements. *SA impact: delivery includes user documentation, not just a model endpoint.*

**5. Human oversight**
High-risk systems must be designed so that humans can understand, monitor, and intervene. Fully automated decisions without human review are restricted. *SA impact: human-in-the-loop is an architecture requirement — build it in from Phase 1, not Phase 4.*

**6. Accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity**
The system must perform consistently, handle errors gracefully, and be resilient to adversarial inputs. *SA impact: robustness testing and adversarial evaluation must be part of the test plan.*

**7. Conformity assessment**
Before going to market, high-risk systems require a conformity assessment — either self-assessment or third-party audit depending on the category. *SA impact: plan 3–6 months for this process; it gates go-live.*

**8. Post-market monitoring**
Providers must actively monitor deployed systems and report serious incidents to regulators. *SA impact: MLOps and drift monitoring are not optional — they are regulatory requirements with reporting obligations.*

## Key Roles Under the Act

| Role | Definition | SA relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **Provider** | Develops and places the AI system on the market | Bears the heaviest compliance obligations — typically the consulting firm or ISV building the system |
| **Deployer** | Uses the AI system in their business (formerly "user") | Must follow provider instructions, conduct own risk assessment for high-risk systems, ensure human oversight |
| **Importer / Distributor** | Puts a non-EU provider's system on the EU market | Must verify the provider has met obligations |

> When a manufacturer uses a custom-built AI system internally, they are both **provider and deployer** — full obligations apply.

---

## General Purpose AI (GPAI) Models

Foundation models (LLMs, large vision models) have their own rules:

- All GPAI providers must publish technical documentation and comply with copyright law for training data
- GPAI models with **systemic risk** (above a compute threshold: >10²⁵ FLOPs) face additional obligations: adversarial testing, incident reporting, cybersecurity measures
- **SA impact**: If you build a solution on top of a third-party foundation model (e.g., GPT-4, Llama), the GPAI provider handles their tier of obligations — but you as the solution provider are still responsible for the application layer's compliance.

## SA Checklist for Any New Engagement

1. **Classify the risk tier** in Phase 1 — before estimating effort
2. **Identify the role** — is the client the provider, deployer, or both?
3. **Flag high-risk obligations** to the client early — each adds scope and timeline
4. **Design human oversight into the architecture** from the start if high-risk
5. **Scope documentation deliverables** — technical file, model card, instructions for use
6. **Include post-market monitoring** in the MLOps design — it is a legal requirement, not a feature
7. **Assess foundation model risk** if using a vendor LLM — understand what the GPAI provider covers and what remains your responsibility
