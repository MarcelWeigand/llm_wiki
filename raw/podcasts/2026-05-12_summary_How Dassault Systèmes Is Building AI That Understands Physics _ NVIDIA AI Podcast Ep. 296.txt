Welcome to a fascinating exploration into how Dassault Systèmes is pioneering AI that truly understands the physical world around us. In this episode of the NVIDIA AI Podcast, we heard from Olivier Sappin, the CEO of the Transportation & Mobility Industry at Dassault Systèmes, who shed light on their groundbreaking work in fusing artificial intelligence with the immutable laws of physics. Their ambition isn't just to make AI smarter, but to make it scientifically sound and capable of augmenting human ingenuity in ways we’ve only dreamed of. Let's dive into the core insights of how they're achieving this complex but crucial goal.

Dassault Systèmes has a rich history as a leader in 3D design and Product Lifecycle Management, or PLM. They’ve long been instrumental in helping industries create digital representations of products. However, their vision has evolved significantly with the concept of the "virtual twin." This isn't merely a digital model; it's a living, breathing, scientifically accurate representation of a real-world system, whether it’s a car, a city, a human heart, or even the entire planet. These virtual twins are comprehensive, incorporating physics, chemistry, and biology to create dynamic, predictive models that mirror and understand their real-world counterparts. This intricate foundation is absolutely crucial for their AI strategy, as it demands an artificial intelligence that can not only interact with but inherently understand these incredibly complex simulated realities.

One of the biggest challenges facing the application of AI in engineering and science, as Olivier explained, is the inherent limitation of traditional AI, particularly deep learning, when dealing with physical phenomena. While excellent at recognizing patterns in vast datasets, conventional AI often struggles with causality, extrapolation, and understanding fundamental physical laws. Imagine an AI trained solely on data from existing crash tests; it might fail spectacularly if asked to predict the behavior of a car designed with entirely new materials or a different structural integrity, simply because it lacks an inherent understanding of material science, fluid dynamics, or structural mechanics. Physical systems operate under strict, well-defined mathematical laws, which standard AI typically doesn’t incorporate naturally. This can lead to predictions that are statistically probable but physically impossible or nonsensical – essentially, "magic" without a grounding in reality.

To overcome these limitations, Dassault Systèmes is building what they term "physics-informed AI." This groundbreaking approach moves beyond merely training AI on data. Instead, their AI models are explicitly embedded with the equations and principles of physics. They integrate numerical methods, known scientific laws, and decades of engineering knowledge directly into neural networks. This means the AI doesn't have to "rediscover" gravity or electromagnetism; it’s inherently aware of them. This method allows the AI to make robust and reliable predictions, even when data is sparse or when it needs to extrapolate beyond previously observed conditions. It ensures that the AI's conclusions are not just statistically likely, but scientifically plausible and consistent with the fundamental laws of nature, making the AI more trustworthy and reliable for critical applications.

The practical applications of this physics-informed AI are incredibly vast and are already transforming multiple industries. In manufacturing, it optimizes processes, drastically reduces scrap rates, and enables precise predictive maintenance, saving enormous costs and increasing efficiency. In materials science, it accelerates the discovery and simulation of new materials, revolutionizing product development cycles. For the transportation sector, it aids in designing safer autonomous vehicles, optimizing traffic flow, and developing smarter urban infrastructure. The concept even extends to the life sciences, where virtual twins of human organs can test drug efficacy and identify potential side effects virtually, significantly speeding up drug discovery. And on a global scale, Dassault Systèmes' platform is crucial in projects like NVIDIA's Earth-2, helping to simulate and predict complex atmospheric and climate phenomena with unprecedented accuracy.

Crucially, Dassault Systèmes views AI not as a replacement for human intellect, but as a powerful co-pilot. They champion a "human in the loop" approach, recognizing that human engineers and scientists remain critical, especially for tackling novel problems, interpreting complex results, and providing the intuition that AI still lacks. Engineers can leverage this advanced AI to perform incredibly complex simulations, explore vast design spaces, and analyze data at speeds impossible for humans alone, freeing them to focus on creative problem-solving and critical decision-making. This collaborative strategy, further strengthened by partnerships with companies like NVIDIA on the Omniverse platform, democratizes access to incredibly sophisticated simulation and design tools, fostering innovation by making complex scientific computation more accessible and intuitive for a broader range of professionals.

So, to answer the podcast's central question: How is Dassault Systèmes building AI that understands physics? They are achieving this by moving beyond traditional, purely data-driven AI. Instead, they are deeply integrating fundamental physical laws, scientific principles, and engineering knowledge directly into their AI models. By grounding AI in the comprehensive reality of the "virtual twin" and explicitly embedding physics, chemistry, and biology, they are creating intelligent systems that are not only highly predictive but also inherently robust, verifiable, and capable of extrapolating reliable insights in the most complex engineering and scientific domains. This approach is empowering engineers with a scientifically sound AI co-pilot, driving unprecedented innovation and solving some of the world's most intricate challenges, from designing next-generation vehicles to modeling the profound complexities of climate change.
