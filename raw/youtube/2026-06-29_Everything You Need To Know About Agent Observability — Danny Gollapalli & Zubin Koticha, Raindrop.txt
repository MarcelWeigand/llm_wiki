The speaker, Zubin, talks about the challenges of monitoring AI agents in production. He highlights how AI agents are different from traditional software in that they are non-deterministic, unbounded, and tool-using. These characteristics make it difficult to identify and fix issues in AI agents, and the problem is only getting worse as AI agents become more complex and are deployed in high-stakes environments like healthcare and finance.

Zubin argues that traditional evaluation methods (evals) are not enough to monitor AI agents effectively. He proposes a shift from testing to monitoring, which he believes is better suited for catching long-tail issues and allows for faster iteration. He emphasizes the need for good signals to build reliable AI agents, categorizing them into explicit and implicit signals. Explicit signals are objective and verifiable, such as error rate, latency, regenerations, and cost. Implicit signals are more nuanced and deal with the semantic nature of what's happening, including user frustration, refusals, task failures, jailbreaking attempts, laziness, incorrect language, forgetting, and positive wins. He mentions that their product, Raindrop, provides out-of-the-box support for these signals and allows users to set up alerts.

Zubin also discusses the importance of regex as a signal, citing an example from Claude's source code where regex was used to detect negative user prompts. Finally, he talks about the use of experiments in production to test changes and measure their impact on these signals. He demonstrates how Raindrop allows users to track user frustration, aesthetic complaints, and deployment issues, showing how a new prompt version led to significant improvements.

During the Q&A section, a participant asks about the amount of data needed for statistical relevance in experiments. Zubin explains that even a few hundred events can be useful, especially when combined with a good set of signals. He further elaborates that they use trained models to detect implicit signals, making it cost-effective and scalable, and that they support exporting data to other platforms. Another participant asks if these monitoring techniques apply to non-chat interactions, and Zubin confirms that they do, particularly for tool failures and capability gaps, allowing agents to diagnose and report issues autonomously.
